Thoughts on LLMs

March 11, 2026  ·  Haochang Luo  ·  ~8 min read

My experience with today's large language models (LLMs) is that in domains with abundant training data, they demonstrate unparalleled efficiency and accuracy. However, when it comes to specialized scientific fields, taking my own research area as an example, they can be wildly wrong.

I once used Claude to vibe-code a simple app for my iPhone. From frontend to backend, it completed the task almost perfectly. But when I asked it to write a demo for NeuralGCM, the result was riddled with bugs, even inventing functions that do not exist. This clearly illustrates the importance of training data scale. When an LLM has not been fine-tuned in a specific domain, it may produce answers that appear reasonable but are fundamentally wrong. It reminds me of early days of ChatGPT around 2023, when it confidently told me that the Coriolis effect is strongest at the equator.

Given this, it almost seems that the profession most replaceable by LLMs today is software engineering, at least judging from the rising unemployment rate in that field, simply because the training corpus for programming is enormous. This suggests that among different professions there exists a kind of "hierarchy of replaceability." The more procedural and standardized a job is, and the less subjective initiative it requires, the more easily it can be replaced.

From this perspective, fields in the humanities and social sciences (e.g., law, politics, economics) appear to sit near the top of this pyramid. LLMs typically understand logic through contextual relationships. Compared with humanities and social sciences, the contextual structure of code seems much more "flat." Consider an analogy from atmospheric science. The essence of atmospheric motion is the superposition of waves and noise with different frequencies and wavelengths. Therefore, when analyzing the atmosphere we often decompose the system into different scales. If we apply the concept of "scale" to logical context, we might say that the reasoning in humanities and social sciences operates on much larger scales—decades, centuries, even millennia. After filtering out noise, these disciplines summarize patterns across countless individuals.

However, when such large-scale contextual knowledge is fed into an LLM, information may collapse during representation. The model's interpretation becomes biased, and those biases get iteratively amplified, eventually producing answers that are completely mismatched to the question.

This leads to another important issue: how to quantify model uncertainty.

In weather forecasting, uncertainty is commonly quantified using ensemble forecasting. Multiple models with different physical frameworks or parameterizations run forecasts under the same initial conditions. The outputs are then averaged or converted into probabilities. For example, if nine out of ten models predict rain, we say that the probability of rain tomorrow is 90%. If only a single model is available, we instead compare its predictions with its historical performance and use statistical hypothesis testing to estimate confidence intervals, giving us a quantitative measure of how trustworthy the forecast is. However, none of the LLM products currently on the market provide explicit quantitative uncertainty. There may already be relevant research papers, but from a product standpoint, the only thing companies seem to do is add a disclaimer saying "AI-generated content should be used for reference only."

One could imagine applying this "ensemble" concept to LLMs to quantify uncertainty. For example, we could use multiple models from different providers, or multiple versions of the same model with different parameters, to answer the same question, then compare their outputs. But that raises another challenge: how do we quantify the similarity between answers?

If the task is solving a math problem, evaluation is straightforward. But if the output is natural language, we would need to measure semantic similarity. Would that require nesting yet another large model to perform the evaluation? This kind of "judging model" is not unprecedented. Scientists have already been using techniques like Generative Adversarial Network or Random Forest to post-process outputs generated by deep learning models. So perhaps in the future we will see a system where one LLM acts as a judge evaluating the outputs of other LLMs. Maybe OpenClaw is already exploring something like this. I'm not sure yet, since I haven't used it. For now, I'm still looking for a safe and interesting way to experiment with it. We'll see how it develops.

对于现在的大语言模型，我用下来的感受是在训练集充足的领域，它们展现出了无与伦比的效率和准确率，但对于具体的科学领域，就以我自己的研究领域而言，它们会错得离谱。

我尝试用Claude vibe code一个iPhone能用的众包地图类app，从前端到后端，它完美地完成了这个任务。但我让它写一个neuralGCM的demo却是bug满天飞，甚至编出来不存在的函数。所以，这里就可以看出训练集样本数量的重要性。当一个LLM未经过具体领域的fine tuning,它可能会给出一个看似合理但实际上错误的答案（梦回23年chatgpt告诉我赤道地转效应最大)。所以时至今日，似乎LLM最能取代的职业是程序员（至少失业率挺高），因为训练集实在是太大了。

这也说明，在众多的职业中，存在一个"可取代性的等级"。越是接近于流程化规范化的，主观能动性越低的职业越能被取代。从这个角度看，似乎人文社科（e.g. 法律 政治 经济）站在了这个金字塔的顶点。LLM在理解逻辑的时候往往是通过上下文。与人文社科相比，代码的上下文似乎更"扁平" 。不妨做这样一个类比：大气运动的本质是不同频率波长的波动和噪音的叠加，因此我们在分析时往往会把它过滤成不同尺度的系统。如果把"尺度"的概念套到逻辑的上下文上，我们就可以得出，人文社科的逻辑似乎是一个更大的尺度。十年，百年，甚至千年的，过滤掉噪音的对于无数个体的总结，当它作为上下文被输入到LLM中时，信息发生了坍缩，模型的理解产生了偏差，然后偏差被不断迭代，出现驴唇不对马嘴的现象。

这就引出了另一个问题，如何量化模型的不确定性。对于天气预报，我们常常采用集合预报来量化不确定性，即用多个不同模型（不同物理框架或者不同参数）在相同初始条件下进行预报，最后对结果进行加权平均或者直接给出概率。例如，10个模型中有9个报了下雨，那我们就会描述为明天下雨的概率是90% 。如果条件有限，只有一个模型，那就对比它历史预报，用假设检验，计算样本的置信区间来给出有多大概率能相信它的预报。

但现在市面上的LLM，没有一家能给出具体的不确定性，或许已经有相关论文，但就目前的产品来看，他们做的仅仅是在免责条例里添加一句"AI 生成只能作为参考" 。所以接下来可以幻想一种类似于集合预报的方法来定量分析LLM的不确定性，例如用多家模型或者单一家不同参数的模型，让它们对同一问题给出答案，对答案进行对比分析。

但这就涉及到一个问题，如何量化答案的接近程度。如果问题只是计算一道数学题那当然最容易量化，但如果输出的是文本，那就得量化语意，难道要再嵌套一个大模型来做这件事吗。这种套娃结构其实以前也见过，有人用GAN或者random forest 来对深度学习模型产生的结果进行后处理。所以以后会不会出现一个LLM来扮演其他LLM裁判的角色呢。或许openclaw已经在做这件事了？不知道，我还没用，在找一个能安全且有意思的方式使用它。

拭目以待。
